Kitty Oliver lecture: Race historian speaking Saturday in Ormond

 
Erica Van Buren @EricaVanBuren32

Wednesday

Feb 26, 2020 at 10:41 AM Feb 26, 2020 at 10:47 AM


Kitty Oliver is the founder of the Cross Cultural Race and Change Oral History Archive.


ORMOND BEACH — As Black History Month comes to a close Saturday, the Ormond Beach Historical Society will offer a lecture to explore the topic of race and progress.


The lecture is titled "Race and Change Across Cultures and Generations: Florida Stories."


"I think people can expect a surprisingly different approach to the topic using storytelling as well as research information to explore, human beings in dealing with differences," said Kitty Oliver, oral historian.


The lecture will also look at changes in approaches to racial diversity that have evolved over decades, especially in Florida.


Oliver said there has been a transformation in terms of different cultures learning how to interact.


Oliver is the founder of the Cross Cultural Race and Change Oral History Archive. She’s an author, oral historian and media producer specializing in race and ethnic relations.


"I’ve been writing about diversity, for many, many years now in terms of racial interactions and cross cultural interactions," said Oliver. "I began interviewing people as a way to collect their stories of race, ethnic relations and experiences over time."


Oliver, 72, grew up in Jacksonville and said she was one of the first black freshmen to attend the University of Florida in 1965. She earned her bachelor’s degree in English from UF and went on to receive master’s in creative writing from Florida International University and her doctorate in the Comparative Studies Public Intellectuals Program at Florida Atlantic University, focusing on race and ethnic communication.


"From interviewing people and training students we were able to gather what became an archive of 125 interviews, from a cross section of white, black, Hispanic, Asian and Caribbean cultures," Oliver said.


What began as personal exploration turned into a lifelong journey.


"I think that I’m a product of my time," said Oliver. "I came of age when the Civil Rights Act was passed. I was one of those first group of people who walked through that door into the new world."


The free program is family oriented and open to all ages.


"It certainly could be a time for people across generations to learn and see how their history now ties in with the past and that we’re all on the same continuum," said Oliver.
