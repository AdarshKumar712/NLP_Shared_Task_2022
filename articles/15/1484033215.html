Every year has its defining stories.

Some make us think. Some make us laugh. Some make us cry.

The year 2019 was no different, and as it has been since 1874, the St. Tammany Farmer was there with readers every step of the way.

We’ve selected what we think are the Top 10 local news stories from the previous year. While there may be differing opinions regarding which stories should be on the list, one thing’s for certain – there will be many more stories to share in 2020.

Check out this glance back at the Top 10 stories in St. Tammany Parish in 2019 and read along weekly as we report the most important news from around the parish in 2020.

1. Former St. Tammany Parish Sheriff Jack Strain was indicted in June for sex crimes involving underage males, a shocking twist for the former lawman who was already in the sights of federal prosecutors who last year indicted two members of Strain’s inner circle for an alleged kickback scheme.

Skip Keen and David “Doc” Hanson pleaded guilty in February to the kickback arrangement at a work release facility that Strain had privatized in 2013. But it wasn’t until late August that Strain himself was indicted on 16 corruption counts. The link between the two cases turned out to be Keen, who was one of Strain’s four alleged victims.

Strain has pleaded not guilty in both cases. His state trial on eight sex crime charges could begin as early as April 20.

2. Walter Reed, the long-time district attorney for St. Tammany and Washington parishes, finally began a four-year prison term in May, three years after he was convicted in federal court on 16 fraud and corruption charges.

Reed, 73, reported to a federal prison in Morgantown, West Virginia in May after federal prosecutors argued that he had exhausted his appeals. Reed fought to stay out while he appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court, but a federal judge ordered him to jail, where he reported after another delay to allow him to have surgery. The Supreme Court refused to hear his appeal in late June.

3. Mandeville Police Capt. Vinny Liberto was shot to death on Sept. 20, the first officer killed in the line of duty in the city since 1958.

Liberto, a Marine Corps veteran who had been on the force for 25 years, died after he was fatally wounded trying to stop a suspect fleeing an attempted traffic stop. Another officer, Ben Cato, received a graze wound. Thousands attended Liberto’s funeral at the Castine Center, and the city named a street alongside City Hall after him.

Mark Spicer, the 21-year-suspect, was indicted on first degree murder and attempted first degree murder and other charges by a St. Tammany grand jury in early December.

4. St. Tammany voters elected a new parish president, former Covington Mayor Mike Cooper, who received 61% of the vote in a Nov. 16 runoff between him and incumbent Pat Brister.

Cooper, who will be sworn in on Jan. 13, will be the third parish president since the adoption of the home rule charter in 1998. Cooper ran a tireless campaign to defeat the two-term incumbent who was widely viewed as vulnerable following three attempts to renew a pair of sales taxes for the jail and courthouse. Cooper, who served two terms as Covington’s mayor, stressed that he had not raised taxes during his tenure. But he takes office with a looming fiscal cliff caused by the loss of $22 million in revenue that the two taxes brought in. The 2019 budget and the budget for next year, relied in part of fund balances that will run out in 2021.

5. A congested portion of Interstate 12 between U.S. 190 and La. 59 will be widened, with work to begin early next year.

More than 76,000 vehicles per day travel that part of the interstate, which was also the scene of a fiery crash in 2018 that killed four people. The work, estimated to cost $55 million, will include overlaying a 3.26-mile section and adding a lane in each direction. The project was not supposed to start until 2023, but Parish President Pat Brister pushed state and federal officials accelerate the time frame. Its being paid for with federal transportation dollars that were not used by other states and were redistributed to states that had obligated all of their federal highway funding.

Louisiana, which got $76 million from that source, is using $52 million of it for this project. The parish is contributing $8 million. Work is expected to take 2 ½ years.

6. Medline, a global manufacturer and distributor of medical supplies, announced on Dec. 18 it will build an 800,000-square-foot distribution center on a site between Ochsner Boulevard and Interstate 12 south of Covington.

The facility will add 464 new jobs to the parish — 170 within the first two years — with an average annual salary of $37,400 plus benefits, and another 611 indirect jobs would be created, according to a release heralding the planned project.

The announcement was issued the same day St. Tammany Parish President Pat Brister said she would not veto the controversial zoning ordinance that would allow Medline to proceed with its plans. The Parish Council previously had voted 10-2 to OK the ordinance, but Brister was resolute, saying it wasn’t the best spot for the facility. She reversed course, she said, because a super-majority of the Council was planning to override her veto. By not vetoing the ordinance, citizens in the area who plan to file suit in objection to the rezoning could begin that process sooner, Brister said.

Opponents of the project say that it will increase traffic problems in an already dangerously congested stretch of I-12 and will create drainage problems. Homeowner groups and others turned out in force at several meetings to object to changing the site's zoning from a planned business campus to an advanced manufacturing and logistics designation.

Medline, the largest privately held manufacturer and distributor of medical supplies in the United States, began discussions with Louisiana Economic Development in April. The company will receive a $2 million performance grant to defray infrastructure costs and will be eligible for a $500,000 incentive to offset the cost of building a new road to the sit

7. In an era when even tax renewals are heavily scrutinized by voters, a pair of St. Tammany Parish School Board proposals passed with ease in May.

Voters favored nearly 2 to 1 a 2-mill property tax to fund resource officers and mental health providers at the district’s 55 schools. They showed an almost equal amount of support for a $175 million, 20-year bond issue to fund capital improvements at 16 of those schools, and to further technology and security upgrades systemwide.

The bond issue passed 65 percent to 35. The property tax passed 64-34. The Louisiana Secretary of State’s office said 9 percent of parish voters turned out for the election.

The school board passed a measure last year to use money it received in the BP oil settlement to place officers and mental health providers in parish schools to start the 2018-19 school year. The program was organized and implemented in less than six months in the wake of the Parkland, Florida school shooting that claimed the lives of 17 students and staff on Valentine's Day 2018.

Passage of the permanent funding source assured that RHOs and MRPs would remain in place on campuses parish-wide. Both school leaders and law enforcement officials have said having that support in schools has been a proactive tool in addressing potential threats.

The bond issue is set to fund capital upgrades at 16 of the district’s 55 schools, mostly to replace old modular buildings with new classroom space. Of that $175 million, $25 million will be used for systemwide security and technology upgrades. The bonds will be paid with revenue from 13.90 mills that the district already levies.

8. A feasibility study commissioned by the St. Tammany Parish Tourist and Convention Commission determined that a 150-room hotel and conference center should be developed at Fontainebleau State Park near Mandeville.

The notion of a hotel amid the green space of Fontainebleau was soundly rebuffed by people from around the parish, but the prospect remains on the table.

The commission hired Pinkowski & Co. to conduct the study, at the request of Lt. Gov. Billy Nungesser. Results of the study, which were announced at the tourist commission’s June meeting, said a lodge and conference center would generate $1.8 million in cash flow in 2021, its first year, growing to nearly $2.3 million by 2025.

Opponents said the study itself noted that some existing businesses would be hurt by building a hotel/conference center in Fontainebleau. They also said if the hotel pays no property taxes because it is on state property, that the number of campers and persons renting cabins on site could diminish.

A public meeting on the matter is scheduled for Jan. 8 at the Mandeville Community Center. Nungesser is expected to appear.

9. The St. Tammany Parish School Board voted in February to ask Sharon Drucker, its District 9 member, to resign in the wake of her arrest for shoplifting $58.25 worth of merchandise from a Walmart near Covington.

Drucker, who represents the Madisonville area, was polite but defiant in the face of her colleagues' displeasure, saying she was "steadfast" in her determination to stay in office. Voters returned Drucker to the School Board with 69 percent of votes only weeks after the charges surfaced.

Drucker pleaded not guilty to the misdemeanor shoplifting charge in October 2018 and was allowed by the 22nd Judicial District Attorney's Office to enter a 12-month diversion program that could lead to dismissal of the charge.

Drucker said she was taking full responsibility for the charge by participating in diversion. Several peers on the board decried Drucker’s decision to not step down, saying she no longer could be an effective leader in wake of the charges. Others came to her defense, saying her public embarrassment was punishment enough.

The government watchdog group Concerned Citizens of St. Tammany called for Drucker’s resignation and the St. Tammany Republican Parish Executive Committee also called for her to consider resigning. Drucker is a Republican.

10. Lakeshore High School quarterback Christian Westcott became the first player in St. Tammany Parish history to be named the Gatorade Louisiana Football Player of the Year.

Westcott, a senior, joined the ranks of football legends such as Peyton Manning and Leonard Fournette in winning the award, which has been presented to the state’s best high school football player for the past 35 years.

The award “recognizes not only outstanding athletic excellence, but also high standards of academic achievement and exemplary character demonstrated on and off the field.”

Westcott completed 151 of 275 pass attempts for 2,779 yards and 35 touchdowns this season, and he rushed 198 times for 1,368 yards and 38 more scores. He also had returned an interception for a touchdown while playing defense and registered 20 tackles for loss, 6.5 sacks and forced four fumbles.

Lakeshore reached the Class 4A state semifinals and finished with a 13-1 record in 2019.

Compiled by Andrew Canulette and Sara Pagones.
