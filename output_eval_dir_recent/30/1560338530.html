    Policy support being stepped up

    Emerging Asia Chart Book
    26 March 2020
    Written by Gareth Leather

    With economic growth across the region collapsing, policy support is being ramped up aggressively. Having slashed interest rates over the past couple of weeks, central banks have begun to unveil a series of unconventional policy measures to support activity and ease strains in the financial markets. In the Philippines, the central bank has announced that it will begin purchasing 300bn peso (approximately 1.5% of GDP) in bonds directly from the government, while the Bank of Korea today adopted its own version of QE, whereby financial institutions will be able to borrow unlimited amounts of money from the central bank at an interest rate of 0.85%. Fiscal support is also being scaled up. Singapore today announced a second supplementary budget worth around 8% of GDP, which in addition to the measures unveiled in February’s budget, takes the total support package to 11% of GDP. Korea is also putting the final touches together of a deal set to be worth 5% of GDP. While policy loosening won’t prevent a sharp economic slowdown in the first half of the year, it should ensure that the region bounces back strongly if/when the virus is contained.

Korea is also putting the final touches together of a deal set to be worth 5% of GDP. While policy loosening won’t prevent a sharp economic slowdown in the first half of the year, it should ensure that the region bounces back strongly if/when the virus is contained.
    • Coronavirus Infection Figures. Confirmed cases of the coronavirus have surged in South and South East Asia in recent days, but there has been some good news to report. The number of cases in China has dropped to close to zero, while cases in Korea have fallen sharply. Taiwan, Hong Kong and Singapore have also been successful in “flattening the curve”.
    • Economic Activity. A collapse in tourism, restrictions on the movement of people, the closure of non-essential shops and a sharp fall in exports will weigh heavily on economic activity over the coming months. We think most economies will contract this year and that regional GDP will fall by more than it did during the global financial crisis.
    • External. Early indicators already point to a sharp fall in global trade. Our forecast is that global trade will fall by around 25% this year. This will act as a major drag on the region’s most trade-dependent economies, namely Singapore, Malaysia and Vietnam.
    • Inflation. Falling currencies and disruptions to manufacturing production will put upward pressure on prices this year. However, with oil prices down over 50% since the start of the year and economic growth slowing sharply, we think inflation will fall across the region this year.
    • Policy Support. Policy is being eased aggressively. Most countries have cut interest rates and further cuts are likely. Fiscal policy has also been loosened. Debt levels across Emerging Asia are relatively low, which means most countries could afford further stimulus to support demand.
    • Financial Markets. Financial markets have plummeted over the past month. Equity markets have dropped everywhere, with those in South and South East Asia experiencing the biggest pummelling. Currencies have also fallen, with the Indonesian rupiah experiencing the biggest drop. Financial markets are only likely to experience a sustained rebound when there is clear evidence the outbreak is being contained.

    Coronavirus Infection Figures

    • The region has seen a surge in the number of coronavirus cases over the past couple of weeks. South East Asia and South Asia appear to be the worst affected (1). While the number of confirmed cases in these regions still appears relatively low, the high mortality rates suggests that the official numbers are a significant underestimate (2). Lockdowns have been imposed in most countries, but weaker governance and less access to healthcare mean that the virus will be harder to bring under control.
    • There has, however, been some good news to report. The number of confirmed cases in China has slowed dramatically. Korea, which has the highest number of confirmed coronavirus cases per inhabitant of any major country in Emerging Asia (3) also appears to have contained the virus. There were 104 new cases on Thursday, down from a peak of over 800 at the start of March (4).
    • Other countries that were initially worst affected by the virus but have also now managed to “flatten the curve” are Hong Kong, Taiwan and Singapore (5). Although these countries have seen a jump in cases over the past few days as citizens have returned from the US and Europe. Contact tracing, targeted quarantine measures and a strong public response have all been essential in helping to control the spread. A high incidence of testing in order to catch infections early on has also been crucial (6).

    Economic Activity

    • The outlook for the region has worsened dramatically. We expect economic activity to plummet over the coming months. Tourism across the world is grinding to a halt (7) as more and more countries advise their citizens against overseas travel and close their borders to international visitors. Cambodia and Thailand are likely to the worst affected (8).
    • Domestic demand will be hit hard as authorities impose tough new restrictions on the movement of people, and shops and restaurants are ordered to close. Investment will also decline as businesses react to the uncertainty by slashing planned capital expenditure programmes. We have little in the way of hard data for March, but the collapse in the February PMIs for China (9), which imposed a lockdown on economic activity in late January, provide a taste of what we can expect for the rest of the region.
    • An economic recovery will not happen until the virus is brought under control. China and parts of South East Asia are likely to see the biggest declines in GDP this year, while very few places will record positive growth (10). In the most vulnerable economies, notably Thailand, economic activity could fall by 8% q/q in the first quarter alone (11). In aggregate terms, we think Emerging Asia (excluding China and India) will contract by 1%, which would be worse than during the global financial crisis (12).

    External

    • The timely nature of the Korean trade data makes them a good bellwether for the health of trade across the world. In working-day adjusted terms, exports fell by 0.4% in the first 20 days of March. In levels terms, exports bounced back a little from last month but remained low (13). There were not any obvious signs of a big slump in demand from Europe as the coronavirus forced countries there into lockdown. Imports also recovered slightly. The disruption from Chinese factory shutdowns looks to have eased (14).
    • Looking ahead however, the stabilisation in Korean trade is only likely to be temporary. Measures of global trade that we track on a daily basis are already pointing to a sharp downturn. The average ship speed of large container ships has slowed (15), while the cost of shipping goods has also fallen sharply (16).
    • Our forecast is that the global economy will contract by around 2.5% this year, which is an even bigger downturn than recorded during the global financial crisis. This is likely to lead to a slump in the region’s exports (17). The most trade dependent economies, namely Singapore, Malaysia and Vietnam, will experience the biggest hit (18).

    Inflation

    • The latest data show that even before the crisis erupted, inflation in most countries was already very low (19). Disruptions to the supply side of the economy and a fall in regional currencies will put upward pressure on prices (20), but this is likely to be outweighed by other factors.
    • Oil prices have dropped by over 50% from their peak at the start of the year. This will cause energy price inflation to fall sharply (21), and by our estimates will knock over 1.0%-points off headline inflation. In addition, we expect most economies in the region to contract this year, putting further downward pressure on prices. Core inflation dropped sharply during the global financial crisis (22).
    • While inflation is likely to slow everywhere, a number of countries are likely to experience deflation (23). A temporary period of deflation is nothing to worry about. Over the last few years a number of countries, including Thailand and Taiwan, have experienced short periods of deflation with few ill effects (24). However, there is a risk that deflation becomes more entrenched. As the example of Japan shows, when this happens, deflation can be hard to get out of.

    Policy Support

    • In response to the dramatic deterioration in economic prospects, most countries have unveiled fiscal stimulus packages (25). The biggest response far has been in Singapore. Thailand and Hong Kong have also announced sizeable packages. Debt levels across Asia are lower than in the rest of the world (26), and most countries have room to loosen policy further. One exception is Indonesia, where the constitution prohibits the government from running a budget deficit greater than 3% of GDP. High bond yields in Sri Lanka (27), mean they have limited scope to loosen policy and may even be required to tighten policy.
    • Interest rates have also been reduced aggressively (28), and further easing is likely. The key exception is in Indonesia, where a slump in the rupiah (29), means the central bank will have to pause its easing cycle. Policy rates in Thailand and Korea are below 1.0% and they have limited room to cut rates further (30).
    • In addition to interest rate cuts, central banks have been engaged in unconventional policy measures to calm financial markets. The central bank in Indonesia has been buying government bonds on the secondary market to support the currency. While in the Philippines the central bank has purchased 300bn peso (approximately 1.5% of GDP) in bonds directly from the government.

    Financial Markets

    • Emerging Asian equities have rebounded slightly in recent days but are still down around 30% since the start of the year (31). Foreign investors have been selling off Asian equities in record amounts (32). Pakistan and India have experienced the biggest stock market falls, closely followed by Indonesia and the Philippines (33).
    • Currencies have also fallen, although in most cases the declines have been more modest (34). The exception is the Indonesian rupiah, which is down nearly 15% over the past month and is at its weakest level relative to the US dollar since the Asian financial crisis (35). A big current account deficit which is funded largely through volatile portfolio flows makes the currency vulnerable to periods of financial stress.
    • US dollar yields have spiked even as the risk-free rate has dropped back. The spread on the JP Morgan EMBI+ Asia index has increased by nearly 250bps since the start of the year (36). It is now at its highest level since the Asian financial crisis.
