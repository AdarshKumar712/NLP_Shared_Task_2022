    Swiss KOF Economic Barometer (Jan.)

    Nordic & Swiss Data Response

    A positive start to the year

    • The surge in the Swiss KOF Economic Barometer in January may yet prove to be a bit of a rogue reading. Nonetheless, it adds to signs from elsewhere in Europe that conditions for industrial firms have stabilised at the start of the year, and will probably calm nerves at the SNB.
    • The rise in the Barometer, from a downwardly-revised 96.2 in December, to 100.1 in January was the biggest monthly increase since May 2015. The outcome was much stronger than we and the consensus had pencilled in and left the indicator above its long-run average for the first time in 16 months. The Swiss franc rose slightly against the euro after the release but has since edged down again.
    • The size of the increase has a whiff of a rogue reading about it. Indeed, the Barometer has tended to track the euro-zone Composite PMI in recent years, which was unchanged in January. (See Chart 1.) It’s possible that the KOF has been skewed by a jump in sentiment after the Phase One trade deal between the US and China, in which case we may see it fall back a bit in February.
    • Nonetheless, the fact that the increase was driven by a broad-based improvement in the manufacturing sector adds to the evidence that the industrial sector is now past the worst. This ties in with the rise in the Swiss manufacturing PMI in January, which rose above the 50-mark for the first time in nine months, and a similar jump in Sweden. Recent data suggest that the German manufacturing recession is easing too.
    • On past form the KOF now points to annual Swiss GDP growth of above 1%y/y in mid-2020 (see Chart 2), so the risks to our below-consensus 2020 forecast of 0.8% now seem to be on the upside. Perhaps more importantly, signs that the economy is proving resilient may give the SNB the confidence to dial back its FX interventions (see Chart 3) and to let the franc appreciate.
