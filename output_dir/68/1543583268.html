    Overview

    Oil prices to recover lost ground

    • We forecast that oil prices will recover from their recent losses and rise by the end of this year in part because we expect the market to move into a small deficit. In contrast, we think that huge market surpluses will continue to weigh heavily on the average prices of natural gas and coal. (See Chart 1.)
    • Despite the easing of US-China trade tensions, the rise in US-Iran geopolitical frictions and a slight weakening in the US dollar (see Chart 2), energy prices have slumped in the last few weeks. Warm weather in the Northern Hemisphere weighed on coal and natural gas demand. Elsewhere, the spread of the Wuhan coronavirus generated renewed investor pessimism in the oil market. China is a much bigger consumer of oil products now than it was during another comparable epidemic (SARS in 2003). (See Chart 3.)
    • Provided that the virus is eventually contained, we expect a pick-up in global economic growth to put a floor under energy prices later this year. Stronger economic growth should also boost global stock markets and risk appetite in general, which will be particularly beneficial for oil, the most highly traded energy commodity.
    • Otherwise, geopolitics will remain a key driver of energy prices in 2020 and 2021. The US-Iran dispute is one of the most pressing issues currently and, while there is not much room for Iranian crude oil exports to fall further (see Chart 4), any rise in tensions is an upside risk to our oil and natural gas price forecasts.
    • Further ahead, we think that decarbonisation will be the main factor affecting energy prices and that coalâ€™s role in electricity generation will continue to diminish over the coming decades.

    Economic and Financial Backdrop

    Nearing a trough

    • Since the end of 2017 world GDP growth has slowed from 4% to just below 3%. This was due to a confluence of negative shocks and the lagged effects of policy tightening in some of the worldâ€™s largest economies. But as the drag from these shocks has faded, and as the worldâ€™s central banks shifted into loosening mode, the stage is now set for a revival in global growth, with inflation and interest rates remaining low.
    • Throughout 2018 and the first half of 2019, the economic data just kept on getting worse. But since the end of last year there has been growing evidence that the world economy is finally finding its feet. (See Chart 5.)
    • The bottoming out or outright improvement in the surveys and hard data hasnâ€™t been shared by all though. Indeed, the stabilisation of some global indicators masks some important pockets of weakness. For instance, Japan looks to have gone into recession following a sales tax hike in October. In Germany, some business surveys are consistent with the economy remaining in contractionary territory.
    • However, given the synchronised loosening of monetary policy last year (see Chart 6), financial conditions have become much more accommodative, which should help support a gradual pick-up in global growth. Much of the emerging world has already embarked on cyclical recoveries, and we think that advanced economies will join them, led by the US.
    • Because turns in the business cycle only affect underlying prices with a lag, the economic slowdown from the past two years has yet to fully drag on core inflation. According to its lagged relationship with the output gap in advanced economies, core inflation is on course to fall to 1.5%, on average, by the end of this year. (See Chart 7.)

    Economic and Financial Backdrop (continued)

    • Working in the opposite direction, higher oil prices should boost energy inflation (see Chart 8), which should offset the drag on headline inflation from lower core rates. Lower food inflation will weigh on overall price pressures in emerging markets (EMs) in 2020, but headline inflation in developed markets (DMs) should generally change little from current rates.
    • Last year, falling inflation was key for sustaining decent growth in real incomes in advanced economies as jobs growth slowed. Consumers will not benefit from lower inflation in 2020, and labour markets are still weakening after the slowdown in 2019. So, with real growth in labour incomes likely to ease slightly in the coming year, a marked turnaround in consumption growth is unlikely. (See Chart 9.)
    • Instead, a rebound in investment growth will probably lead the recovery. With trade war uncertainty diminished after the Phase One deal, commodity prices rising, financial conditions looser, central banks keeping interest rates low, and businesses set to use up spare capacity, the conditions for an investment recovery are in place. Higher public investment will support growth in some cases. But, in general, there wonâ€™t be much fiscal loosening.
    • All in all, we think that Q4 2019 marked the trough in global GDP growth and expect it to recover gradually over the course of 2020-21. (See Chart 10.) The rebound should be driven principally by DMs. Growth in EMs should also rise a bit, as recoveries in most of the emerging world just about offset a property sector-led slowdown in China. (See Chart 11.)
    • Having contracted in 2019, world goods trade should grow again in 2020-21. Note, though, that part of the upturn in trade growth is down to base effects. (See Chart 12.)

    Oil

    Prices to rise in 2020

    • We forecast that the prices of Brent and WTI will surge to $75 and $72 respectively by the end of this year owing to stronger demand growth, constrained supply and a pick-up in investor risk appetite.
    • Oil prices rose in the final months of last year on the back of the easing of US-China trade tensions, the escalation of US-Iran geopolitical tensions and OPEC+â€™s decision to deepen its output cuts. However, they have fallen sharply since the middle of January 2020 as the Wuhan coronavirus led to fears that transport demand will drop sharply. For now, we are assuming that the virus will eventually be contained and that oil prices will still increase by the end of this year (see Chart 13) for three key reasons.
    • First, we anticipate that the new maritime shipping rules (IMO 2020) will structurally increase demand for low-sulphur distillates by around 0.5m bpd, which corresponds to around 0.9m bpd of additional crude demand. We also expect demand growth to be supported by a modest rise in global economic growth. (See Chart 14.) In total, we estimate that crude oil demand will grow by just over 1m bpd in 2020, compared to our estimate of 0.8m in 2019.
    • Second, we expect supply growth to be constrained this year. In the US, we think that weak capital investment last year will dampen output growth. Meanwhile, we expect OPEC+ to continue to move closer to its output quotas (see Chart 15) and to also roll them over until the end of this year.
    • Putting supply and demand together, we estimate that the market will be in a small deficit this year. (See Chart 16.) The deficit could even be bigger if OPEC+ decides to deepen the output cuts as hinted by a few of the members.

    Oil (continued)

    Risk appetite to return to oil prices

    • Third and assuming that the coronavirus is brought under control, we expect risk appetite to return to financial markets as fears about a possible global recession subside. This should help to remove the price discount that we estimate is currently incorporated into prices. (See Chart 17.)
    • Indeed, if oil prices were based solely on supply and demand (fundamental) factors â€“ rather than influenced by the optimism and pessimism of investors â€“ then our in-house net-demand oil proxy suggests that oil prices would be much higher today. (See Chart 18.) In fact, we estimate that the price of Brent would currently be trading at around $70 per barrel if fundamental factors alone were incorporated into the price. (See Chart 19.)
    • One upside risk to our price forecasts is if Iran attempted to blockade the Strait of Hormuz (the worldâ€™s busiest oil transit route for oil). If this were to happen, prices would skyrocket. However, we think that any price impact would be short-lived as high global stocks could be drawn down (see Chart 20) and supply networks would quickly adjust.
    • Meanwhile, the biggest downside risk is that the world economy enters a recession, perhaps as a result of coronavirus. Although, this would probably only delay, rather than derail, a pick-up in growth.
    • Turning to 2021, we forecast that the price of Brent and WTI will climb a little higher. This is premised on our forecast that global economic growth will be stronger and lead to an increase in risk appetite as global stock markets rise. However, we expect the Brent-WTI price spread to drop a touch as US exports continue to grow.

    US & European Natural Gas & LNG

    A surfeit of supply

    • An unusually mild start to the Northern Hemisphere winter was the nail in the coffin for natural gas prices at end-2019. (See Chart 21.) Looking ahead, we expect prices to stabilise at these low levels in 2020, before rising a little in 2021 on the back of slower growth in supply.
    • In the US, the number of rigs drilling for gas has slumped and current prices suggest that rigs could fall further. (See Chart 22.) The collapse in gas prices has undermined producersâ€™ profitability and is disincentivising investment.
    • Indeed, production eased back in January. (See Chart 23.) Whatâ€™s more, US oil drilling rigs have also dropped back, which points to lower growth in associated gas production. Admittedly, US gas output is still at a historically high level, but we forecast that growth in supply will slow sharply this year.
    • Given that new LNG export capacity is set to come on stream in 2020 (see Chart 24), we think that US demand for natural gas will hold up better than elsewhere. However, falling gas prices in the EU and Asia will act as a ceiling on US prices. We have pencilled in a fairly flat Henry Hub price at an average of US$2.3 per mBtu this year, virtually unchanged from $2.5 in 2019.
    • Turning to Europe, the price of natural gas (TTF) has also fallen on the back of the mild start to winter and more than comfortable supply. The news that Russia agreed a new five-year gas transit deal with Ukraine added to the downward pressure on prices.
    • We expect the average price of TTF to remain low in 2020 at â‚¬11.8 per Mwh for two main reasons. First, stocks are high (see Chart 25) and will probably continue to build this year as the euro-zone economy remains in the doldrums.

    US & European Natural Gas & LNG (continued)

    Prices should pick up a little in 2021

    • Second, the region is awash with supply given the rapid growth in global LNG exports and the prospect of greater accessibility to Russian gas via Nordstream 2.
    • Elsewhere, in January, Asia spot LNG prices lurched lower amid plentiful supply and soft demand in the traditional markets in Asia.
    • On paper, the Phase One trade deal should provide a fillip for US exporters as China pledged to significantly increase its energy imports from the US. However, the 25% import tariff on US LNG remained in place and, in any case, growth in Chinaâ€™s gas imports has slowed sharply. (See Chart 26.) Whatâ€™s more, import growth could fall further in early 2020 given that economic activity will be sharply lower owing to the Wuhan coronavirus.
    • Given sluggish import demand, it therefore seems likely that any Chinese buying of US LNG will be at the expense of imports from elsewhere. This is particularly the case as the Siberia pipeline, bringing Russian gas to China, became partly operational in late 2019. Pulling all this together, we expect spot LNG prices to average just US$5 per mBtu this year, down from $5.5 in 2019 and $9.7 in 2018.
    • By 2021, we expect a modest revival in global economic growth, lower growth in LNG exports (see Chart 27) and further efforts to decarbonise to provide a more positive backdrop for natural gas markets.
    • On decarbonisation, Germanyâ€™s coal consumption has already fallen sharply, mostly thanks to rapid growth in renewables. (See Chart 28.) But the country is also phasing out nuclear power, so we think natural gas will still have a role to play in the medium term.

    Coal

    Hard to be positive on Pacific coal

    • We expect average coal prices to fall this year owing to subdued demand in coal consumers and ample supply, which will cause the market surplus to widen. (See Chart 29.) Ultra-low natural gas prices and efforts at decarbonisation will be additional factors weighing on prices.
    • After falling sharply in the first half of last year, Pacific coal prices have remained low in recent months, despite some sign of recovery in Chinese manufacturing activity (see Chart 30) and the northern hemisphere winter. Instead, ample supply and the low price of gas, a cleaner alternative to coal in power generation, weighed on coal prices.
    • Looking ahead, we expect coal prices to remain subdued. For one, we think that the price of Asian LNG will remain low (see Chart 31), which will continue to weigh on coal prices.
    • Moreover, coal demand is unlikely to stage a recovery. Indeed, we expect economic activity in China, by far the worldâ€™s biggest coal consumer, to slow this year. This should weigh heavily on coal demand and offset any boost to global consumption from a recovery in growth in India and other parts of Asia. Moreover, policies to curb pollution and decarbonise will increasingly chip away at Chinaâ€™s coal use.
    • At the same time, supply is likely to remain ample. Chinaâ€™s production looks set to expand further due to improved transport links. (See Chart 32.) India has plans to ramp up domestic production rapidly and Russia also intends to increase exports to the Pacific region, to deepen its economic ties with China.
    • The upshot is that we expect the price of Pacific coal to average just $65 per tonne this year, down from $78 in 2019.

    Coal (continued)

    European coal prices to remain grounded

    • We forecast that the average price of European coal (Rotterdam) will fall to $50 per tonne in 2020, down from $62 in 2019.
    • This winter, the Rotterdam coal price has so far failed to benefit from the usual seasonal uplift. Instead, the price has continued to fall owing to a combination of warmer-than-usual winter weather in Europe, weak economic activity (see Chart 33), and low natural gas prices.
    • We think that average prices for European coal are likely to remain low for the rest of 2020 for two key reasons. First, we expect strong growth in gas supply to lower the average price of European gas (TFF). As gas is a substitute for coal, this should put further downward pressure on coal prices. (See Chart 34.)
    • Second, we forecast that demand will soften. Part of this is because of ongoing decarbonisation, but it is also due to our below-consensus forecast for real GDP growth in the EU to slide to just 0.7% y/y in 2020 from 1.2% in 2019. We anticipate that the economic slowdown will be particularly sharp in Poland and Germany â€“ both of which are major coal consumers. (See Chart 35.)
    • Longer term, we think regional consumption is in structural decline. Many countries (including Germany) have promised to phase-out the resource completely within power generation.
    • Additionally, the new European Commission has plans to include the maritime and construction sectors into its Emissions Trading System (ETS), maybe from around 2025 onwards, given the time taken to implement new legalisation. (See Chart 36.) Consequently, we expect there to be fewer carbon allowances in circulation. This will put upward pressure on carbon prices and help to incentivise the use of alternatives to coal within power generation.

    Long-term Oil Outlook

    Oil prices to fall in real terms

    • Subdued global GDP growth and rapidly rising fuel efficiency mean that growth in demand for oil will slow over the next decade. Whatâ€™s more, we think demand will peak in the 2030s and fall thereafter, owing to the ongoing electrification of the transport sector, subdued growth in global trade and concerns about the impact of fossil fuel use on climate change. At the same time, plentiful oil reserves mean that supply should be ample. As a result, we expect real oil prices to trend down from the early 2020s to 2050. (See Chart 37.)
    • Soft global economic growth will also weigh on oil demand. (See Chart 38.) This is particularly the case as growth is likely to be driven more by services and technology, which are typically not as oil intensive.
    • There is more than enough oil in reserves to meet this level of demand. In the next decade, we expect steady increases in shale oil production in the US. Supply will become more responsive and OPEC will continue to lose market share. (See Chart 39.) However, in the second half of our forecast period, we see the re-emergence of OPEC, and especially the Gulf producers, as the principal source of supply. They are by far the lowest-cost suppliers.
    • Declining demand, ample output and lower marginal costs should all put downward pressure on oil prices. We estimate that the real Brent price will fall to $47 per barrel (in 2019 prices) by 2050, from around $60 today.
