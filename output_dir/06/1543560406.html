    Further slowdown in 2020 with coronavirus a new threat

    China Economic Outlook
    23 January 2020
    Written by Mark Williams
    Cancel X

    Cooling property construction is likely to weigh on economic activity in the coming quarters. But with external demand starting to stabilise, the slowdown should be gradual, as long as the coronavirus outbreak is contained.

    Pdf
    Save to Library
    • Overview – Cooling property construction is likely to weigh on economic activity in the coming quarters. But with external demand starting to stabilise, the slowdown should be gradual, as long as the coronavirus outbreak is contained.
    • External Demand – The Phase One trade deal marginally improves the outlook for China’s exports. More important is that the global economy appears to be stabilising, which should put a floor beneath export growth in the coming quarters.
    • Domestic Demand – A weak labour market means that consumers will remain cautious, even if the coronavirus is contained. Meanwhile, slower property construction will weigh on investment growth.
    • Inflation – Consumer price inflation should drop back sharply in the coming quarters as disruptions to pork supply continue to fade and broader price pressures remain muted.
    • Monetary & Fiscal Policy – With the economy not yet out of the woods, policymakers still have more work to do. Given that fiscal policy is already very loose, we think the balance of stimulus in 2020 will shift towards monetary policy.
    • Financial Stability – Slower growth means that corporate balance sheets will remain under strain in 2020. The risk of this triggering financial instability has been reduced by policymakers’ willingness to step in and bail out troubled lenders. But in the process, they have reinforced the assumption of state support, which will perpetuate the problem of poor resource allocation.
    • Property Sector – Property controls are likely to remain tight across much of the country in the near term, keeping sales growth subdued. Construction activity is starting to cool in response, with a further slowdown on the horizon.
    • Hong Kong – Following a sharp downturn in the second half of last year, Hong Kong’s economy is showing signs of improvement. But while fiscal stimulus, fewer protests and improving export conditions should continue to drive a recovery, GDP is unlikely to rise across 2020 as a whole.
    • Long-term Outlook – China’s economy faces structural headwinds from slowing productivity gains and a shrinking labour force. With policymakers showing little appetite for the reforms needed to boost productivity growth, we expect the trend rate of economic growth in China to more than halve over the decades ahead.

    Overview

    Further slowdown in 2020 as coronavirus emerges as a new threat

    • Cooling property construction is likely to weigh on economic activity in the coming quarters. But with external demand starting to stabilise, the slowdown should be gradual, as long as the coronavirus outbreak is contained.
    • GDP growth slowed by half a percentage point in 2019, according to the official figures. They have not always been a good guide to cyclical trends. But the modest deceleration is plausible. Our China Activity Proxy (CAP) suggests that growth was slower in 2019 than the official figures show but more stable. (See Chart 1.) This may reflect a skew towards heavy industry, which outperformed last year.
    • One of the main headwinds in 2019 was weaker external demand. The Phase One deal removes the risk of further tariff escalation but won’t boost growth since it leaves US tariffs high. In any case, weak exports were mainly the result of cooling global demand, not the trade war. We expect export demand growth to bottom out soon. (See Chart 2.)
    • With the disruption to pork supply from African Swine Fever starting to abate, the drag on consumption from higher inflation should ease before long. (See Chart 3.) Car sales may also pick up this year as the hangover from the removal of earlier tax breaks fades. However, a sustained turnaround in domestic demand looks some way off. The IT sector remains a drag, with the recent tech boom still unwinding. Lacklustre credit growth and strained corporate balance sheets will weigh on investment. The coronavirus outbreak has emerged as a new concern, primarily for sectors related to travel and tourism. The official GDP figures didn’t show it, but these sectors caused a dramatic, although short-lived, weakening during the SARS outbreak. (See Chart 1 again.)
    • As things stand though, the biggest threat remains real estate. Property construction accelerated last year, shoring up heavy industry. (See Chart 4.) But unless developers accelerate the launch of new projects, which seems unlikely given tighter access to financing and subdued sales, then construction will soon slow as the flurry of projects that broke ground in 2018 are completed. (See Chart 5.)
    • As a result, we still expect growth to decline in the coming quarters. But with growth holding up better recently than we had been expecting, growth this year will be stronger than we had previously anticipated. We now expect growth on the CAP to slow from 5.5% in 2019 to 5.0% this year (up from 4.5% previously) before stabilising in 2021. Growth on the official figures will be higher and edge down by a smaller margin. (See Chart 6.)
    • This slowdown will keep policymakers in easing mode. The budget deficit looks on course to widen further, though by less than in 2019. (See Chart 7.) We think, contrary to the consensus, that the balance of stimulus will shift towards monetary policy. We expect the PBOC to step up cuts to the rates on its lending facilities. (See Chart 8.)
    • Softer growth and lower interest rates are likely to weaken the renminbi. But with the outlook improving, we have adjusted our end-2020 USD/CNY forecast from 7.50 to 7.20.
    • Further ahead, the economy faces structural drags from slowing capital accumulation, a shrinking labour force and gradual decoupling from Western economies. With the leadership showing little appetite for the kind of market-based reforms that would raise productivity growth, we expect economic growth in China to fall to 2% by 2030.

    External Demand

    Headwinds to trade starting to fade

    • The Phase One trade deal marginally improves the outlook for China’s exports. More important is that the global economy appears to be stabilising, which should put a floor beneath export growth in the coming quarters.
    • Exports stagnated in 2019, expanding just 0.5% in dollar terms. US tariffs received much of the blame and it’s true that shipments to the US contracted sharply last year. But a weaker renminbi offset part of this drag. And many exporters circumvented the tariffs by shipping goods via third countries. Exports to ASEAN jumped last year, much as they did during previous efforts to evade US tariffs on washing machines and solar panels. (See Chart 9.)
    • The Phase One trade deal won’t change this dynamic since it leaves most US tariffs in place. It reduces the near-term risk of a further escalation in trade tensions. But with tariffs less of a drag than many believe, the impact on the outlook will be small.
    • Rather than the trade war, the main driver of weaker export growth over recent quarters has been the slowdown in global demand. In fact, China’s share of global exports has increased since the first US tariffs were introduced, implying that the country’s exporters have weathered the slowdown in global trade better than those elsewhere. (See Chart 10.)
    • The good news is that the global economy appears to be stabilising. GDP growth among China’s major trading will probably remain sluggish this year, but it won’t slow much further and should start to recover heading into 2021. As a result, growth in Chinese export volumes looks set to bottom out in the coming quarters. (See Chart 11.)
    • Meanwhile, after contracting 2.8% in 2019 imports should return to growth this year. The drag on imports from the US due to retaliatory tariffs started to ease in Q4 as China made some goodwill purchases of US goods in the run-up to the trade deal. But as with exports, much of the drag from tariffs was offset by a pick-up in imports from elsewhere, most notably Brazil. (See Chart 12.)
    • This reshuffling of suppliers will reverse this year. Imports from the US will almost certainly jump, even if they fall short of the ambitious targets set out in the Phase One deal. (See Chart 13.) But this will inevitably come largely at the expense of imports from elsewhere.
    • Instead, the main reason to think imports will fare better this year is that they were unusually weak relative to economic growth in 2019. This was partly due to the inventory cycle in the electronics sector which now looks to be turning. Import growth rebounded sharply at the end of 2019, on the back of stronger shipments of integrated circuits and semiconductors. We think this tailwind, along with favourable base effects, will only be partially offset by weaker demand for commodity imports as a result of slower economic growth. (See Chart 14.)
    • Higher import price may also shore up growth in import values. (See Chart 15.) In particular, our commodities team forecast oil prices to rise to $75 per barrel by year end, on the back of recovering demand and tight supply.
    • With import growth likely to outpace export growth this year, the current account surplus looks set to shrink, reversing much of its rise in 2019. (See Chart 16.)

    Domestic Demand

    Consumption growth to remain sluggish

    • A weak labour market means that consumers will remain cautious, even if the coronavirus is contained. Meanwhile, slower property construction will weigh on investment growth.
    • Retail sales have continued to slow in recent quarters, with growth especially weak in real terms. (See Chart 17.) Some of the headwinds that weighed on consumption last year are now easing. After more than doubling due to African Swine Fever, pork prices have now started to drop back. Assuming this continues, inflation will decline sharply in the coming quarters, reducing the drag on real incomes. Meanwhile, car sales should return to growth this year as the hangover from earlier tax breaks, which brought forward demand in 2016/17, fades. (See Chart 18.)
    • But despite these props, consumer spending is likely to remain sluggish this year. Sentiment is downbeat – households are reporting an increasing preference for saving, and optimism about the outlook for house prices has waned. (See Chart 19.) And, unlike last year, there are no major tax cuts planned that households can look forward to.
    • Consumers will probably remain cautious as long as the labour market stays weak. The surveyed unemployment rate for major cities is at a record high (albeit for a series with a short history) and is likely to remain elevated this year. (See Chart 20.)
    • The coronavirus outbreak is an additional risk. The government is being more proactive in trying to limit its spread than was the case with SARS. These measures, as well as people wanting to avoid crowds, will significantly disrupt travel and spending during the Lunar New Year holiday and possibly well beyond. Passenger traffic could contract abruptly. However, if SARS is any guide the impact will be short-lived. (See Chart 21.)
    • Another drag to services activity may come from the IT and telecommunications sector. It boomed from 2015 to 2018, thanks to the rapid growth of mobile payments and sharing economy platforms. But with adoption slowing, this tech boom began to unwind last year. (See Chart 22.) The downward trend is likely to continue for a while.
    • The outlook for investment growth is mixed. Capital spending among manufacturers has been unusually weak recently relative to capacity utilisation rates and should recover this year as trade tensions fade and exports stabilise. (See Chart 23.) However, given weak profit growth and tight financing conditions for private firms, a strong rebound is unlikely.
    • Infrastructure spending may also pick-up modestly. We expect local governments to issue a third more special purpose bonds than they did last year. And they will be required to spend a larger share of the proceeds on infrastructure projects.
    • However, this is unlikely to fully offset the pullback in property construction that is just getting underway. (See Chart 24.) Even if the pace of new housing starts remains stable, growth in floor space under construction will slow in the coming quarters as the flurry of projects that broke ground a couple of years ago are completed.
    • The upshot is that domestic demand looks set to remain a drag on economic growth this year, even as external demand starts to stabilise

    Inflation

    Turning a corner

    • Consumer price inflation should drop back sharply in the coming quarters as disruptions to pork supply continue to fade and broader price pressures remain muted.
    • Consumer price inflation jumped to an eight-year high in 2019. This was entirely driven by higher food prices. Oil prices were lower last year than in 2018. And core inflation, which strips out both food and energy, has edged down in recent quarters. (See Chart 25.)
    • The main culprit was disruptions to pig supply caused by African Swine Fever (ASF), which led to a jump in pork prices and also pushed up the price of other proteins such as beef.
    • The good news is that authorities appear to be making headway in halting the spread of ASF. After shrinking by almost half, the number of pigs in China started to recover in the final months of last year. (See Chart 26.) As a result, pork prices began to decline coming into 2020. (See Chart 27.)
    • Although ASF could stage a comeback, it seems likely that we have passed a turning point and that pork prices will continue to drop back in the coming quarters as pork production rebounds. With the base for comparison also set to rise, we think headline inflation will drop back sharply as a result. (See Chart 25 again.)
    • Just as concerns over ASF have started to fade, officials are facing the spread of another virus, this time infecting humans. But if the SARS pandemic in 2003 is any guide, coronavirus is unlikely to have a big impact on prices. Inflation was broadly stable during the outbreak and only picked up once the economy rebounded. (See Chart 28.)
    • Higher oil prices will probably put some upward pressure on inflation this year. (See Chart 29.) Although oil prices have dropped back in recent weeks, we think that they will rise to $75 per barrel by year-end on the back of tight supply and a recovery in demand.
    • By pushing up input costs for firms, higher global commodity prices should also nudge up producer price inflation. But with cooling property construction likely to weigh on industrial demand and domestic steel prices, factory-gate price pressures will remain muted. (See Chart 30.) This suggests that core consumer price inflation, which has followed producer price inflation closely in the past, is also likely to remain low in the coming quarters. (See Chart 31.)
    • One specific factor that will help to keep a lid on core inflation this year is official efforts to address rapid rises in healthcare costs. After surging in 2017, medical services inflation has since dropped back. But officials have now turned their attention to drug prices and are using their power over the drug approvals process to push pharmaceutical companies to cut prices. (See Chart 32.)
    • The upshot is that, after receiving more attention than usual last year, inflation is likely to become much more benign again in 2020. By the middle of the year, consumer price inflation will probably have fallen back below the PBOC’s 3% annual target. This will give the central bank fewer reasons not to loosen monetary policy.

    Monetary and Fiscal Policy

    Rate cuts still on the table

    • With the economy not yet out of the woods, policymakers still have more work to do. Given that fiscal policy is already very loose, we think the balance of stimulus in 2020 will shift towards monetary policy.
    • The Ministry of Finance (MoF) has done much of the heavily lifting to stimulate the economy in recent quarters. It enacted the biggest tax cuts in over a decade, totalling around 2% of GDP. (See Chart 33.) And it increased local governments’ special bond issuance quotas by 60% last year. Growth in land transfer revenue also remained strong in 2019, after picking up in 2018, which helped to shore up fiscal spending.
    • As a result, the budget deficit (adjusted for land sales) increased the most since 2015 last year to the largest on record. The deficit is likely to widen a bit further this year as MoF raises the special bond quotas yet again. But the appetite for another round of tax cuts and large jump in the deficit seems limited. And the prop from land sales is also likely to ease. (See Chart 34.)
    • With the scale of fiscal loosening likely to be smaller this year than last, we think more of the burden of responding to a further slowdown in economic growth with fall on the People’s Bank (PBOC).
    • This probably means a couple more cuts to the required reserve ratio to ensure ample liquidity in the banking system. (See Chart 35.) And in order to guide interbank rates lower without undermining its open market operations, the PBOC will need cut the rates on its lending facilities. We expect 50bps of cuts to the PBOC’s MLF and reverse repo rates this year. (See Chart 36.)
    • On past form, lower interbank rates will help to nudge down bank lending rates. (See Chart 37.) If anything, the revamped Loan Prime Rate (LPR) benchmark introduced last year should make loan pricing more responsive to bank funding costs than in the past.
    • Lower lending rates will take some pressure off bank borrowers but will have a muted impact on credit creation. Bank loan growth tends not to respond much to shifts in monetary policy and is instead constrained by quantitative factors such as loan quotas.
    • And unlike previous cycles, the impact of monetary easing in boosting non-bank borrowing has been lacklustre recently. (See Chart 38.) Wider credit spreads on the back of rising corporate bond default are partly to blame. (See Chart 39.) Meanwhile, tighter regulation has led to a pullback in bank lending to non-bank financial institutions, curtailing the availability of shadow credit. (See Chart 40.)
    • These headwinds now appear to be easing slightly, which means that credit growth may start to become more responsive to monetary conditions again. But the factors dampening the monetary transmission mechanism are unlikely to dissipate overnight. As such, a pronounced pick-up in credit growth of the kind seen during previous easing cycles still seems unlikely.
    • The upshot is that while we think the PBOC will loosen monetary policy by more than most expect this year, we expect this only to underpin a stabilisation of growth rather than spark a marked recovery.

    Financial Stability

    Bank bailouts paper over the cracks

    • Slower growth means that corporate balance sheets will remain under strain in 2020. The risk of this triggering financial instability has been reduced by policymakers’ willingness to step in and bail out troubled lenders. But in the process, they have reinforced the assumption of state support, which will perpetuate the problem of poor resource allocation.
    • China’s financial markets have gyrated in recent quarters in response to the twist and turns in the trade war. But with tariffs on hold thanks to the Phase One deal, markets are likely to start to focus more on other developments.
    • We think growth and interest rate differentials will both move against the renminbi this year, as a further slowdown in China triggers more PBOC rate cuts than markets currently expect, while a recovery in the US keeps the Fed on hold. Monetary easing may act as a prop to domestic equity prices, but weak earnings will constrain the pace of gains. (See Chart 41.)
    • Our forecasts are for moderate renminbi depreciation. A sharper move, perhaps in response to worries about the growth outlook, would weigh on risk appetite globally. But it shouldn’t threaten financial stability in China. Foreign currency debt has risen in recent years, but it is still worth less than 10% of GDP, well below the EM average. Foreign currency debt is covered more than twice over by the PBOC’s FX reserves. (See Chart 42.)
    • The key financial risks in China instead have their origins in domestic debt and, in particular, the impact a slowing economy would have on corporate balance sheets. Admittedly, the share of bad loans on banks’ books is broadly stable. And, after jumping last year, corporate bond defaults have dropped back recently. But there are still signs of balance sheet strains, including a rising share of loss-making industrial firms. (See Charts 43 & 44.)
    • Property developers are a weak link given the scale of their borrowing over the past decade and the poor structural prospects for property demand. Spreads for lower rated property developers remain high. (See Chart 45.)
    • Regional lenders are the most at risk from corporate defaults. Unlike the large national banks, their NPL ratio has climbed in recent years. (See Chart 46.) They also have the weakest capital buffers, lowest return on assets and smallest loan loss provisions.
    • However, the assumption of state support means that even the most over-extended regional lenders are able to roll over their funding at affordable rates. This state backstop was briefly called into question last year when regulators tried to impose some losses on the creditors of the insolvent Baoshang Bank. But in the end, they backed down, repaying most creditors in full. And when a handful of other regional banks subsequently ran into problems, they stepped in to avert losses.
    • After jumping in response to the failure of Baoshang, the premium that lower-rated banks need to pay when issuing NCDs, has since dropped back. (See Chart 47.) This helps to reduce the near-term risk of contagion spreading to other lenders. But it also suggests that little progress has been made in weakening the assumptions of state support that distort credit allocation. Near-term stability may therefore come at the cost of a further rise in the debt-to-GDP ratio (see Chart 48) and weaker potential growth.

    Property Sector

    Construction boom going into reverse

    • Property controls are likely to remain tight across much of the country in the near term, keeping sales growth subdued. Construction activity is starting to cool in response, with a further slowdown on the horizon.
    • New home sales stagnated in 2019 despite a loosening of monetary conditions and decline in interbank rates. (See Chart 49.) The key reason is that unlike during previous easing cycles, property controls have remained tight – mortgage rates and down-payment requirements have hardly declined at all.
    • Hopes for a wholesale relaxation of property controls in response to economic weakness are likely to continue to be disappointed. If anything, policymakers appear intent on keeping their grip on the sector tight by instructing banks to curtail mortgages and clamping down on borrowing by real estate developers. As a result, lending to the sector is slowing. (See Chart 50.)
    • This reflects concerns about debt risks among property developers, which are among the most highly leveraged firms in China. It is also a response to the rapid pace of property price growth, which policymakers are still struggling to rein in. (See Chart 51.) Affordability has now stopped deteriorating but has yet to see much improvement. (See Chart 52.)
    • There are few signs of an imminent rebound in home sales. If anything, the near-term risks are skewed to the downside, especially given the coronavirus outbreak. With sales likely to remain subdued, we think that developers will continue to slow the pace of new housing starts. (See Chart 53.)
    • Admittedly, the housing market doesn’t look as oversupplied as a few years ago. (See Chart 54.) But with their finances under increasing strain, developers can ill afford a renewed jump in inventories.
    • Growth in total floor space under construction accelerated last year but has started to edge down. (See Chart 55.) This trend will intensify in the coming quarters as the flurry of projects that broke ground a couple of years ago are completed.
    • Once prices stabilise and the risk of a renewed supply glut diminishes, regulators may become more willing to relax property controls, which would trigger a recovery in sales.
    • That said, we think any rebound in property sales is likely to be smaller than in previous cycles. Policymakers have become wary about the negative consequences of aggressive stimulus. They are also trying to take some volatility out of property prices by discouraging speculation and expanding the rental market.
    • Meanwhile, the property sector faces mounting structural headwinds from demographics and slowing urbanisation. These suggest that underlying demand for new urban housing will decline in the coming years. (See Chart 56.)
    • For more details on the outlook for the property sector see our latest China Property Handbook.

    Hong Kong

    Headwinds from protests, trade and coronavirus collide

    • Following a sharp downturn in the second half of last year, Hong Kong’s economy is showing signs of improvement. But while fiscal stimulus, fewer protests and improving export conditions should continue to drive a recovery, GDP is unlikely to rise across 2020 as a whole.
    • Hong Kong entered a recession in the second quarter of last year. While the y/y contraction in GDP will probably have deepened in Q4, we think the contraction in seasonally-adjusted q/q growth will have eased. (See Chart 57.)
    • Retail sales growth has also begun to show signs of bottoming out. (See Chart 58.) This suggests that consumption expenditure – which makes up two-thirds of GDP – began recovering last quarter despite the continuing protests.
    • What’s more, protests have become less disruptive recently. Unless they flare up again, the supply-side drag on GDP – from strikes and blocked transportation infrastructure – should ease this quarter.
    • The demand-side damage from the protests will take longer to reverse, however. Visitor arrivals both from China and elsewhere have slumped since the protests began. (See Chart 59.) We had been anticipating that non-mainland arrivals would normalise once the protests subsided. But visitors from the mainland make up a large majority of arrivals and they were likely to be more wary given how intensely state media have condemned the protests.
    • The coronavirus outbreak casts a deeper shadow over the outlook. The drop in visitors from the mainland due to the protests may lower the chances of widespread contagion of the coronavirus. But Hong Kong’s economy is still particularly vulnerable, as was the case with SARS, if locals and outsiders alike take precautions to avoid crowds. It is a transport hub, a travel destination in its own right, and retail spending is a key part of the economy. Activity will be weak over Chinese New Year and until the outbreak is contained. One other lesson of SARS though is that the damage should be transient. (See Chart 60.)
    • More positively, the government recently announced that its budget deficit will be close to 3% of GDP for the fiscal year that ends in March. This is the first time the government has run a deficit since 2003 and a big change from the prior fiscal year, when the government ran a surplus equal to 1% of GDP. The territory’s very strong fiscal position means policy could be loosened further if the economy fails to recover.
    • The headwinds to Hong Kong’s trade logistics sector – which generates almost a fifth of GDP – have lessened recently as shipments to China have picked up. What’s more, export growth now looks unusually weak relative to global GDP growth and should rebound before long. (See Chart 61.)
    • The extremely high level of house prices relative to earnings and rents remains a concern. (See Chart 62.) But sentiment in the property market appears unshakable and we see no immediate catalyst for a correction. While equity and currency markets came under pressure last year, property prices fell only slightly despite the protests which may have been expected to dent the confidence of mainland property buyers. (See Charts 63 & 64.)

    Long-term Outlook

    From growth star to middle-of-the-road EM

    • China’s economy faces structural headwinds from slowing productivity gains and a shrinking labour force. With policymakers showing little appetite for the reforms needed to boost productivity growth, we expect the trend rate of economic growth in China to more than halve over the decades ahead.
    • China’s public capital stock per capita is above what even the most investment-intensive economies have sustained at similar income levels. (See Chart 65.) Front-loading of infrastructure building has propped up growth in recent years but is generating diminishing returns and cannot be continued indefinitely.
    • Other forms of investment won’t pick up the slack. Slowing urban household formation means that fewer homes will need to be built each year. And with China’s share of global exports unlikely to rise much further, the manufacturing sector will be unable to continue adding factories at the same rate as in the past without creating overcapacity.
    • China’s shrinking pool of labour will become an increasing headwind to growth. (See Chart 66.) Attempts to boost the birth rate in recent years have so far had little effect. And China is too big to lean heavily on immigration to supplement its workforce. We expect the drag from this to peak slightly above 1%-pt in the 2030s.
    • Policymakers could counter these drags through market-based reforms to boost productivity growth. But policy under President Xi has favoured tightening political control and cementing the role of state firms, which means that resource allocation is likely to become less efficient.
    • China’s rising debt burden is also a major barrier to reform since there is a growing risk of financial instability if state support were withdrawn. In theory, the government could provide a temporary back-stop while the financial system adjusted to a new policy regime. But a smooth transition to market pricing of credit risks will be increasingly challenging to pull off.
    • Most likely, the financial system will continue to direct a disproportionate amount of credit to favoured firms. This will worsen resource allocation and drag productivity growth down further.
    • Climate change may also become a growing headwind, as rising sea levels threaten China’s heavily populated coastal cities and rising temperatures lead to a higher incidence of disease. The World Bank estimates that a 3˚C temperature rise would drag China’s GDP growth down by a 1%-pt.
    • All told, we think China’s growth rate will slow from around 5% currently (on our estimates) to about 2% from 2030 onwards. (See Chart 67.) In other words, China will fall off the path of rapid development laid down by Japan, Korea and Taiwan. Instead, it will start to resemble most middle-income EMs which have converged with developed economies at a much slower pace, if at all. (See Chart 68.)
    • But the renminbi may not follow the path of the “average” EM currency, which typically depreciates in nominal terms due to high inflation. In China’s case, continued overinvestment should keep inflation low and a small but persistent current account surplus means that the renminbi is more likely to strengthen than weaken.
